The Great Masters

Three titans who shaped the Renaissance and changed art forever

The High Renaissance witnessed the emergence of three extraordinary artists whose genius transcended their time. Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo Buonarroti, and Raphael Sanzio—each brought unique vision and unprecedented mastery to their craft, creating works that continue to inspire and captivate the world centuries later.

Raphael Sanzio

1483 - 1520

Painter, Architect

Raphael was an Italian painter and architect during the Renaissance, renowned for his clear forms, harmonious compositions and Neo-Platonic ideals. He was known as one of the "Three Great Masters" along with Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo. Influenced by Perugino in his early years, he went to Rome to work for the Pope in 1508.

He completed important commissions such as the Vatican Frescoes Room, with "The School of Athens" standing as his masterpiece. Though he died young at 37, Raphael produced a rich collection of works characterized by grace, beauty, and perfect balance.

Notable Works

  • The School of Athens
  • The Sistine Madonna
  • The Transfiguration
  • La Fornarina

Leonardo da Vinci

1452 - 1519

Polymath, Painter, Engineer, Scientist

Leonardo da Vinci was a polymath and artist during the High Renaissance, proficient in painting, engineering and science. His notes cover studies in anatomy, astronomy, botany, and more, embodying humanistic ideals. He proposed concepts such as aircraft and armored vehicles, which have had a profound influence on later generations.

His insatiable curiosity and keen observation of nature led him to create works of unparalleled technical skill and emotional depth, forever changing the course of Western art.

Notable Works

  • Mona Lisa
  • The Last Supper
  • Vitruvian Man
  • The Virgin of the Rocks

Michelangelo Buonarroti

1475 - 1564

Sculptor, Painter, Architect, Poet

Michelangelo was a sculptor, painter, architect and poet during the High Renaissance. After the age of 30, he mainly created in Rome and was deeply influenced by classical art. He completed the magnificent frescoes "Genesis" and "The Last Judgment" on the Sistine Chapel's ceiling, works that showcase his unparalleled understanding of human anatomy and divine proportion.

In his later years, he served as the architect of St. Peter's Basilica. His stunning style influenced later generations and promoted the development of Mannerism.

Notable Works

  • David
  • Pietà
  • Sistine Chapel Ceiling (Genesis)
  • The Last Judgment
